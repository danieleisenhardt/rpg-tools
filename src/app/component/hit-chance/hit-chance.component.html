This Page's Purpose

Multiple options for determining a chance to hit

OGL D20 System

This system was published by wizards of the coast in 2000 under the Open Game Licence. It's basis is trying to roll a D20 over your target's Armor Class (AC), which is a combination of the armor your target is wearing and their ability to dodge.

The score of the D20 roll can be augmented by character strength for melee (dexterity for ranged attacks), character level (depending on character class), and many other factors such as spells, magic weapons and allies assisting you in your action. If I had tried to model them all, the calculator below would not fit on the page.

This system may be pretty OK for a pen and paper RPG where combat is not the main aspect of the game, but for a combat-focussed computer game I have many objections to it:

  • Low level characters can be killed by a single unlucky dice roll
  • High level characters end up swinging at each other for a long time
  • It is a one-dimensional system, which lumps ability to dodge and armor protection into one category. Forcing players to maximize both in order to get the best survivability.
  • A lot of tacked-on mechanics are required to give the player any agency beyond:
    'swing weapon at target, again'
  • Anything the player can do to improve their attack roll or armor class can be weighed against the opportunity cost of making another attack. This leads to a singular optimal strategy.

Skill v.s. D100

Inspired by the game Battle Brothers, this system has the attacker roll a D100 under their attack. The opponent's defense lowers the number they have to roll under.

hit chance = skill - defense

Hit chance is always at least 5% and never more than 95%

Even if a high level character has an attack skill over 100, the target's defense skill can still mitigate the hit chance below 95%. This makes this simple system scale well in my opinion.

Attack v.s. average

I'm not sure where I got this from, but I have used it in the past.

hit chance = attack / (attack + defense) * 100

This system gravitates too heavily towards a 50% hit chance in my opinion. For example: any attack has a 33% chance against a defender with twice the skill. This could work at low levels where a large relative differences in skill depending on player choices, but at high level all opposed skills checks will be close to 50/50.

Attack v.s. average squared

This is another system I've tried in the past in an attempt to mitigate the downsides of the attack v.s. average system.

hit chance = attack2 / (attack2 + defense2) * 100

It makes for better character variety at higher levels, but the problem remains: the higher the levels the smaller the difference. This problem could be mitigated further by increasing the exponent, but this will make accuracy swing wildly at lower levels.

Furthermore, this system makes it hard for players to understand what benefit they will get from spending their skill points on attack, defense or something else.

Open-ended 2d6

I know this system from the game Dominions 5, but I have it on good authority that it did not originate there.

To roll an 'open-ended' d6: roll a six sided dice, but when you roll a six change it to a five and roll another six sided dice. Theoretically you could roll an infinitely high number this way, but the chance of that is infinitely small.

This system simply compares the attack and defense stats of the combatants and adds 2 open-ended d6 rolls to them. The attack succeeds if the attacker has a higher number than the defender.

if (attack + 2d6 open ended > defense + 2d6 open ended) {{'{'}}
// hit
{{'}'}}

I like the elegance and of this system as six sided dice are readily available and easy to understand. However it is developed for a game where 7 attack or defense is considered very low, and 16 is only extremely high. Magical equipment can increase scores significantly, but you won't get far beyond 30. With numbers much higher than this a battle can become very one-sided. Or you could easily have two characters that can fight each other forever without either ever hitting if both put a lot of points in defense.

It would require other game systems like fatigue and 'harassment' penalties to mitigate these limitations.
